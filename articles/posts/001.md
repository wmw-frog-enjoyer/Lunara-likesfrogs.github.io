# WHY ARE THERE SO MANY STREAM SLOPS?

I was playing some customs earlier, and as we all know, the customs website is infested with stream slops. However, I noticed a new feature was added: we can now filter out patterns. As someone tired of playing only stream slops, I filtered out streams, copied the most popular maps, and guess what? MORE STREAM SLOPS! I thought this was happening because the maps were categorized as an unknown pattern, meaning the mapper didn't select anything when uploading their custom. So, I was still getting stream slops, but from even lazier mappers who didn't bother to tag the pattern. To solve this, I thought filtering out streams and selecting jumps would eliminate both tagged streams and the unknown pattern issue. But guess what? I WAS WRONG AGAIN! I WAS STILL GETTING STREAM SLOPS!

The more I play customs, the more I realize the proportions stream slops have taken; they went from being every other map to almost every single one on the website. I have seen many different opinions on what a stream slop is and how to solve them. I wanted to share my perspective as someone who doesn't play as much as they used to, along with a quick theory on why we see so many in Sound Space custom maps.

# What are stream slops?

I would first like to clarify the definition I will be using in this video. Some people think stream slops are any map with streams, while others only qualify maps with a continuous stream from start to finish. I personally take a midpoint on that matter.

For me, a stream slop is a map where all the drops consist solely of streams that lack variety compared to other stream slops, apart from their BPM. I personally consider both stream maps and wall maps to be stream slops.

# Why do stream slops exist?

Before getting into my theory, I will give some insight into the debate on why stream slops exist and whether they are good or bad for the community.

Nowadays, we have seen a massive influx of new players. The game hits around 250 players at peak hours, and a couple of months ago, it even reached 400. As the community grows, we naturally start seeing more new mappers. Many veteran mappers have been active since at least 2023, when Sound Space Plus released; because of their experience, their maps tend to be highly polished.

There is a topic about more experienced mappers leaning towards tech maps because they have already mapped a lot of the more generic patterns, but this isn't really the point I'm trying to make right now.

With this influx, many beginners started making less polished content by copying and pasting the same patterns throughout their maps. They often just flip them or make minor adjustments so the map isn't entirely identical. I have done this in my own maps (though I'm not really a mapper and never made stream slops), so I can't blame them for using these shortcuts.

The issue doesn't begin when beginners create unpolished or simple maps; that is completely normal and expected. They aren't going to make super complex tech maps right away. The real problem arises when the only pattern they choose is the one with the least variety, leading to such an overwhelming number of these maps that finding anything other than streams becomes difficult.

Streams are not diverse patterns. The most basic ones are clockwise, counterclockwise, vertical, and horizontal—and that's pretty much it. While there are screw jacks, spins, and other categories, the four basic variations are what we mostly see because they are the easiest to map. In contrast, even simple jump configurations, like star patterns, feel much more unique. I might be missing a couple of sub-patterns since I'm not a mapper, but as a player, I can confidently say that streams tend to feel identical across every single map, with BPM being the only major difference.

# Are stream slops good for the game?

Beginners tend to prefer streams over jumps because a single string of notes is easier to read and follow. Similarly, novice mappers favor streams because they can just rotate, copy, and paste a few sections to finish their maps. Casual players also lean towards simpler patterns like streams, as they can enjoy them without needing to grind or learn complex mechanics. So, if people enjoy playing and creating them, are stream slops really an issue?

I would personally say that they are, for a couple of reasons—some more personal, and some more general.

1. Mappers take longer to learn how to polish their work: When beginners exclusively map stream slops and players actively engage with them, it heavily incentivizes the creator to keep doing the same thing. This creates a repetitive loop, trapping mappers in a comfort zone they never want to leave.

2. Players take longer to learn new patterns: While inactive veteran players don't really need to learn anything new, beginners certainly do. Because streams are easier, new players fall into the exact same comfort zone as the mappers, significantly delaying their ability to learn other patterns.

3. It hurts map discoverability: The sheer volume of stream slops makes it difficult for players wanting to break out of their comfort zone. You might argue that they can just filter out streams and filter in tech. However, doing so hides good tech maps that happen to include tech streams and fails to filter out mislabeled stream slops tagged as tech. Ultimately, it just makes finding diverse maps a hassle.

4. Good streams get buried: Not all streams are stream slops. You can still have diversity within stream-focused maps by using unique designs, like quantum streams, or by mixing in other patterns. Just because a map's main focus is streams doesn't mean it can't include jumps or slides to make it more engaging. The flood of stream slops makes it much harder to find these genuinely good stream maps.

5. Streams are boring to play and watch: When the only difference between maps is the BPM, the gameplay gets incredibly repetitive. Newer players might not mind it yet, but veterans generally tend to heavily dislike stream slops for this exact reason.

6. They create a false perception of good mapping: When beginners join the community and browse customs, all they see are stream slops, leading them to assume this represents quality mapping. It's not about them refusing to leave a comfort zone; they simply don't know any better. New mappers learn by observing others, and seeing mostly repetitive maps encourages them to copy those exact ideas. This cycle makes it incredibly hard for them to discover what polished and varied mapping actually looks like.

# How did stream slops come to be?

This is just a theory on why beginners specifically gravitate toward streams. I believe the problem started between the releases of Rhythia Online and Steam Rhythia. When Rhythia Online first launched, legacy maps were the best way to farm RP. There were very few ranked maps, and the existing ones didn't reward much. As the game evolved, more maps were ranked, and the RP algorithm changed, introducing a clear bias where streams began rewarding significantly more RP than any other pattern.

My personal skillset focuses primarily on jumps, though I am also good at tech and quantum maps. Despite my playstyle being the furthest thing from stream slops, I still gain way more RP by playing streams than anything else (that said, I haven't played Cunev's game in months).

When players realize streams give more RP, they naturally play them more often. Mappers notice this trend and begin creating more of them in response. Since the Sound Space and Rhythia communities overlap heavily, Rhythia mappers also upload to Sound Space, explaining the massive influx of stream slops in our customs.

# Conclusion

At the end of the day, I don't think streams are inherently bad. Some of my favorite maps feature them, and they absolutely have a place in the game. The issue isn't their existence; it's their sheer dominance crowding out everything else.

When players focus on streams, mappers cater to that demand. Consequently, newcomers start to believe that streams are all there is to the game. This fuels a cycle of repetitive content, hindering the growth of other patterns and mapping styles.

Maybe my theory on their origin is completely wrong, but one thing is undeniable: if you browse customs today, you'll find significantly more stream slops than you would have a couple of years ago. Whether that's a good or bad thing is up to you. Personally, I would much rather see a customs page filled with a diverse variety of patterns, rather than endless stream maps that only differ by BPM.
